# Content Power Engine

Most content plans answer: "What should I post?"

This one answers: "What does my audience need to believe before buying feels obvious — and exactly how do I get them there?"

That's the difference between content that builds an audience and content that builds a business.

This skill does two things in one pass:

1. **Strategy layer** — maps the belief gap between where your audience is and where they need to be, then builds four content pillars that close it.
2. **Execution layer** — for each content pillar, produces a writer-ready brief so anyone (or any AI) can execute without rewriting the direction twice.

The output is a single, shareable document. The strategy and the briefs live in the same file. No hand-off gap. No lost context.

---

## Core Principle

**A great brief makes great writing inevitable. A great strategy makes a great brief possible. Most people have neither.**

If you are outsourcing content and getting flat results, the brief failed — not the writer. If you are posting consistently and not moving buyers, the strategy failed — not the effort. This skill fixes both at the root.

---

## Step 1: Get the Offer Intelligence

Three ways this starts:

**URL provided**: Fetch the page with WebFetch. Extract: offer name, who it's for, what it costs (or investment level), the transformation delivered, how it's delivered (live, intensive, course, community, done-with-you), the creator's unique mechanism or framework, and any objections addressed on the page.

**Description provided**: Work with what you have. If the audience, transformation, or delivery method is unclear, ask once — not a list of questions, one message, 2-3 specifics max.

**Vague request**: Ask for the offer in plain language. Three things minimum: who it's for, what it does for them, how it works. One ask. Move fast.

Once you have what you need, extract these five before writing anything:

**The Audience**: Who specifically. What they're already doing. What level of awareness they're at.

**The Transformation**: Before state to after state — written plainly, not as marketing copy.

**The Mechanism**: What makes this work differently than the free or DIY version. This is what separates an offer from a topic.

**The Delivery Format**: Live, recorded, intensive, coaching, community. Why the format matters to the result.

**The Real Objections**: Not "I can't afford it." The deeper story — "I'll figure it out eventually." "I just need better tools." "I'm not that far behind." These are the beliefs that have to break before someone buys.

---

## Step 2: Capture the Creator's Voice

Before building anything, understand who's speaking.

Ask one question: "How would you describe your style when you post? And if you have 2-3 posts that felt most like you, share them."

If they're in a rush or unsure, use defaults: direct, plain language, no hype, leads with point of view. If you have examples, analyze the tone, sentence length, vocabulary level, and whether they lean toward teaching, provoking, or storytelling. Build every angle and every brief to match that voice.

The output should sound like the creator wrote it with strategy behind it — not like a consultant wrote it for them.

---

## Step 3: Map the Belief Gap

This is the strategic core. Everything else follows from it.

**Current Beliefs**: What is this audience telling themselves that makes NOT buying feel reasonable? These are not surface objections — they are the comfortable narratives people use to avoid change. Pull them directly from the real objections extracted in Step 1.

Write them as first-person statements the audience would actually say out loud. "I'm already using AI, I just need to get better at prompts." That's a belief. "They're not tech-savvy enough" is not a belief — it's a category. Get specific.

**Required Beliefs**: What does this person need to believe for buying to feel like the smart, obvious move? Each one should make the offer feel less like a pitch and more like the logical next step.

Write these as first-person statements too. "What I'm doing right now is giving me the illusion of progress, not actual leverage." That's a required belief. "They need to see the value" is not.

The gap between these two sets of statements IS the content strategy. Make this explicit in the report. Every piece of content exists to move someone from one side to the other.

---

## Step 4: Build the 4 Content Pillars

Four pillars. Each does a different job in closing the belief gap.

Rename each pillar based on what it actually does for this specific offer and creator. Make the names feel like the creator's language — not a framework label.

For each pillar, you will:
- Name it and describe its job
- Generate 3-4 specific content angles (named and hooked)
- Produce a writer-ready brief for the pillar (see Brief Format below)

---

### Pillar 1: AUTHORITY (Positioning the Creator)

**The job**: Reframe how the audience sees the creator. Not as someone who teaches the topic. As someone who operates at a level the audience hasn't reached yet. There's a difference between knowing something and running your business differently because of it. This pillar shows the difference.

This is not credential-dropping. It's demonstrated perspective. The creator reveals how they see the world differently because of what the offer is built on. Calm. Certain. Slightly ahead. Not trying to impress.

Generate 3-4 named angles. Each one reveals a gap in how the audience thinks without lecturing them about it. Give each angle a name that describes what it does ("The Operator Standard," "The Pattern Reveal," "The Standard Nobody Talks About"). Include a 1-2 sentence hook for each.

---

### Pillar 2: REALITY EXPOSURE (Dismantling False Beliefs)

**The job**: Show the audience the gap between what they think is working and what actually produces results. Not tips. Not tactics. A perception shift.

This pillar targets the current beliefs from the Belief Gap map directly. It should make the audience think "Wait, that's exactly what I've been telling myself." Then immediately follow that recognition with the reframe.

The tone is not shame. Not superiority. It's what a straight-talking mentor says after they've seen this pattern a hundred times. "Here's what's actually happening."

Generate 3-4 named angles. Each one names the myth or false comfort, then exposes why it's not enough. The hook creates recognition first, discomfort second.

---

### Pillar 3: TRUST BRIDGE (Making Them Feel Seen)

**The job**: Show the audience you understand where they actually are — without letting them stay there. Done right, this pillar does more for conversion than any pitch. It removes the internal resistance that objection handling can't touch.

This is not sympathy that parks someone in their current position. It's empathy that creates momentum. Normalize the struggle. Then redirect toward motion.

Generate 3-4 named angles. Each one meets the audience in their honest moment, names what's really going on, and points toward a different way of seeing it. The creator's own story or past thinking belongs here.

---

### Pillar 4: CONVICTION (Installing the Belief That Makes Buying Obvious)

**The job**: Connect the dots without pitching. The audience has had their worldview challenged (Reality Exposure), been seen (Trust Bridge), and seen the creator operating differently (Authority). Now this pillar makes the offer feel inevitable. Not urgent. Not hyped. Inevitable.

Focus on: the cost of delay, the difference between information and environment, the speed advantage of the right path, or the irreversibility of getting the right framework. The tone is clarity and quiet confidence. No countdown timers in the copy.

Generate 3-4 named angles. Each one leaves the reader thinking "I already know what I need to do." The offer should feel like the obvious path forward.

## Step 5: Writer-Ready Brief for Each Pillar

After generating the angles for a pillar, produce a brief using this format. One brief per pillar. This is the execution document the creator or a writer uses to execute every angle under that pillar without ambiguity.

**Brief Format:**

```
CONTENT BRIEF — [Pillar Name]

PILLAR ROLE: [One sentence on what this pillar does in the content strategy]
PLATFORMS: [Where this content will live — LinkedIn, Facebook, Instagram, X, email, etc.]
FORMAT: [Post type — long-form, short punchy post, thread, email, carousel, etc.]
TARGET WORD COUNT: [Range, not a fixed number — e.g., 200-400 words]

TARGET AUDIENCE:
- Who they are at this stage of awareness
- What they currently believe (pull from belief gap map)
- What they need to leave this content believing

TONE & VOICE:
- Voice description matching the creator's style
- Sentence length and structure guidance
- Words/phrases to use
- Words/phrases to avoid (no filler, no softening, no corporate speak)

CONTENT ANGLES TO EXECUTE:
- [Angle Name]: Hook + 1-sentence direction
- [Angle Name]: Hook + 1-sentence direction
- [Angle Name]: Hook + 1-sentence direction

STRUCTURE:
- Opening: [How to start — tension, observation, question, or direct point]
- Middle: [What the body does — teach, reframe, expose, normalize]
- Close: [CTA or lingering question — no "Hope this helps!" endings]

WHAT NOT TO DO:
- No [specific mistake for this pillar]
- No [specific off-topic tangent]
- Do not resolve the tension with reassurance — push toward motion

SEO / DISCOVERABILITY (if applicable):
- Primary keyword or search angle
- Hashtags or topic clusters for platform

DELIVERY:
- File format (Google Doc, Markdown, plain text)
- Due date if applicable
- Where to submit completed draft
```

**GATE: Every brief must include Tone & Voice, Target Audience, and What NOT to Do. These three sections prevent 90% of revision cycles.**

---

## Step 6: Weekly Posting Rhythm

Map the pillars to a weekly cadence that compounds. The week should create a progression: challenge the worldview early, give a soft landing mid-week, raise the standard late, connect the dots at the end.

Default cadence:
- Monday: Authority (start the week from a position of perspective)
- Tuesday: Reality Exposure (challenge the false belief while attention is high)
- Wednesday: Trust Bridge (meet them where they are mid-week)
- Thursday: Reality Exposure (raise the standard again)
- Friday: Conviction (make the conclusion feel obvious)
- Weekend: Light Authority or conversational (stay present without pressure)

Adjust for the posting frequency the creator wants. Fewer posts = prioritize Authority and Reality Exposure. One post per week = Reality Exposure is the move.

---

## Step 7: The Strategic Logic

Close the report with 3-5 sentences explaining exactly why this plan works for this specific offer. Not a generic "content is important" summary. Connect it back to the Belief Gap. Explain the mechanism. Give the person enough understanding that they can make smart adjustments when the calendar doesn't match the moment.

The goal: they understand WHY this approach converts, not just WHAT to post.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Skipping the belief gap** — Content without a mapped belief gap is just noise. Topics are not a strategy.
- **Generic angles** — If the angle could apply to any offer in any niche, it's not an angle. It's a placeholder.
- **Vague briefs** — A brief that doesn't specify tone, structure, and what NOT to do is not a brief. It's a suggestion. Suggestions get ignored.
- **Sympathy without redirection** — Trust Bridge content that parks the reader in their struggle instead of moving them is not empathy. It's enabling.
- **Pitch dressed as content** — Conviction content that reads like a sales page will be skipped. Inevitability is not urgency.
- **Ignoring brand voice** — Every deliverable must match the creator's voice. A mismatch means the content never gets posted.
- **Hallucinating details** — If a transformation, mechanism, or objection is unclear, ask once. Never invent product features, pricing, or audience specifics.

---

## Recovery & Fallbacks

- **User doesn't know their audience's beliefs**: Ask them for the most common reason someone says "not yet" when offered their program. Work backward from that answer.
- **User can't describe their brand voice**: Ask for 2-3 pieces of existing content they felt proud of. Analyze the voice and describe it back to them before building.
- **User only wants the strategy, not briefs**: Deliver the belief gap map, pillars, and weekly rhythm only. Note that briefs are available when they're ready to execute.
- **User only wants briefs, not full strategy**: Skip straight to Step 5 but require the belief gap inputs (current beliefs, required beliefs) before writing. Brief quality depends on it.
- **User wants a template they can reuse**: Build a master brief template with placeholders for each pillar. Deliver alongside the offer-specific report.

---

## What This Is NOT

Do not produce:
- A generic content calendar with topics that could apply to any offer
- A list of content types ("try carousels, try reels, try polls")
- Tips-based value content that doesn't challenge any belief ("5 ways to use X")
- Positive reinforcement content that lets the audience stay comfortable
- A pitch schedule dressed up as a content plan

The test: every angle in the report directly targets a specific belief from the Belief Gap map. If it doesn't connect to a belief, cut it.

---

## Voice Rules for All Output

These apply to every angle, every hook, and every brief in the report:

- Lead with the point. Never wind up.
- Short sentences hit harder than long ones.
- Plain language beats vocabulary.
- Name the thing directly. No softening.
- Tension before explanation.
- Write how a straight-talking business operator talks — not how a content strategist writes a white paper.
- No corporate filler. No "it's worth noting." No "in today's landscape." No "Hope this helps!"

Match the creator's voice where you have examples. Default to direct and punchy where you don't.
